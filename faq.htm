Q: Why does the Electrocompaniet amplifiers sound like the best combination of transistors and tubes ?

A:Because they are transistor amplifiers designed like a tube amplifier ! The inherent high gain of the transistors is reduced in all stages to a level comparable to tube amplifiers. Using local feedback does this, and in this process linearisation far beyond what is possible with tubes is achieved. The whole thing is neatly packed together with a small but still efficient level of external loop feedback, thus making the amplifier work like a single stage of amplification, and not as N stages cascaded after each other!

Q:How are they designed, and are there several different designs ?

A:All Electrocompaniet amplifiers are based on the same fundamental theory. Today's amplifiers are different from the early designs, but only in implementation, not in how they conform to the theoretical work upon which they are based. The amplifiers are very pure in their design, no electronic tricks are employed. All parameters are carefully balanced against each other. None of the transistors are squeezed beyond their capacity. Careful listening tests combined with constant critique of design rules and measurement results bring the designs forward.

Q:Does loudspeaker cables really affect sound quality? And why ?

A:Five years ago I did not believe this. There seemed to be no natural explanation for this. However, together with a good friend, Paal Rasmussen, we designed a loudspeaker cable, mostly for fun We used every available technique, every method we could think of and tried to eliminate all kinds of believed "illnesses". The cable was very expensive, extremely stiff (not surprisingly - the isolation was more like a solid plastic). All this to "prove" we would not hear any difference. Guess what! We heard a very distinct difference! We measured the cable and noted it was far better damped than any other cable we measured. With a characteristic impedance of only 30 ohm, compared to around 80 for the best of the others. It also had a higher resonance frequency, nearly 13 MHz compared to around 5MHz for the others. Could this be any explanation - well, in fact. We only have theories. The one which seems to fit best is the radio frequency interference (RFI) theory. RFI can be a real killer for an amplifier, causing a lot of inband audio noise modulated onto the audio signal. We call this modulation noise, and it is not easy to measure. In radio equipment you'll pay some 50-100 k$ for this kind of equipment. And they are not designed to measure in the audio frequency range :-(.

Q:Is a class A amplifier better than a class AB ?

A:Yes and No. A class A amplifier uses a higher quiescent current than a class AB. However, all amplifiers will at a given load and signal level turn over to class AB. The problem with many Class A designs is they runs the transistors at a higher current than where they are comfortable. This is a often overlooked fact in audio amplifier design. See my upcoming article "Class AB power amplifier design". In order to design a good class A amplifier you will have to use many transistors in parallel. The optimum current of nearly all good transistors stop at below 1A.
